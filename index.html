What is Money?

What is the role of Money in society?

-

1-. A medium of exchange in economic transactions: Money, created and distributed in physical or digital form by countries is known as currency. Currency is a medium of exchange for goods and services. The United Nations recognized around 180 currencies across the world. The United States dollar is the current reserve currency of the world. Foreign-exchange markets are used to trade exchange rates between two national currencies,

2-. A method of payment: The immediate payment of a buy or sell is known as a cash currency transaction. Goods or services obtained and paid later are known as credit transactions. The buyer on a credit transaction is known as debtor and the seller is known as creditor. The first day after a debtor misses a payment date, the debtor transaction becomes past due, or delinquent.

3-. A unit of account: Money is countable, divisible, and fungible. Countable because it is used to value goods and services and account for profits and losses or credits and debits. To facilitate exchange, money should be divisible to a unit of currency broken down into smaller units. Each unit is fungible, meaning no currency unit of the same type is unique or special and they are all fully interchangeable. For example, there are 5 coins denomination in the US Dollar currency: 1) Penny (valued at 1 cent), 2) Nickel (worth 5 cents), 3) Dime (worth 10 cents), 4) Quarter (worth 25 cents), 5) Dollar (worth 100 cents).

4-. A store of value: The ideal money is the one that preserves its value overtime. It does not depreciate.

Gross Domestic Product

Gross domestic product (GDP) is the total monetary or market value of all the finished goods and services produced within a country’s borders in a specific time period.

GDP = C + G + I + NX

C = consumption or all private consumer spending within a country’s economy, including, durable goods (items with a lifespan greater than three years), non-durable goods (food & clothing), and services.
G = total government expenditures, including salaries of government employees, road construction/repair, public schools, and military expenditure.
I = sum of a country’s investments spent on capital equipment, inventories, and housing.
NX = net exports or a country’s total exports less total imports.

Monetary Base, M1 and M2 Money Stock

-. Monetary base includes M0 plus hard currency held in bank reserves, which is not technically in circulation.
-. M1, a very common definition of money supply, includes hard currency in circulation (M0), plus checking accounts, traveler’s checks, and certain other checkable deposits. Checking accounts, also called demand deposits, are moneys held in a bank, and therefore out of circulation, but the owner can retrieve them from the bank upon demand.
-. M2 includes all of M1, plus savings deposits and CDs. Savings deposits and CDs are slightly less liquid than checking accounts, but can still be converted to cash quickly.

Velocity of Money M1

The velocity of money is the frequency at which one unit of currency is used to purchase domestically- produced goods and services within a given time period. In other words, it is the number of times one dollar is spent to buy goods and services per unit of time. If the velocity of money is increasing, then more transactions are occurring between individuals in an economy.

Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers: All Items in U.S. City Average

The Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers: All Items (CPIAUCSL) is a measure of the average monthly change in the price for goods and services paid by urban consumers between any two time periods. It can also represent the buying habits of urban consumers.
